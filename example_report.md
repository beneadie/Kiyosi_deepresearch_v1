# Comparative Analysis of Investment Philosophies: Buffett, Munger, and Duan Yongping

As of February 2026, the investment landscape has undergone a seismic shift, marked by the official retirement of Warren Buffett and the aggressive integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into global markets. This report provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of the investment philosophies of Warren Buffett, the late Charlie Munger, and Duan Yongping. It explores how the foundational principles of value investing have been preserved at Berkshire Hathaway while being dynamically adapted by Duan Yongping for the technology sector and the unique regulatory environment of China.

## I. Core Principles: Interpretations of Value and Moats

The shared lineage of these three investors begins with the Benjamin Graham school of value investing, yet their modern applications diverge significantly based on their specific "Circles of Competence." While Buffett and Munger spent decades refining the "wonderful business at a fair price" model, Duan Yongping has extended this into high-growth technology, viewing "product power" as the ultimate arbiter of value.

### 1. The Evolution of the 'Moat' Concept
Warren Buffett’s traditional definition of a moat—a sustainable competitive advantage that protects a business from competitors—has historically focused on brand power (Coca-Cola) and low-cost operations (GEICO). However, by early 2026, Buffett’s interpretation has evolved to include "information provenance." His Q4 2025 entry into *The New York Times* (NYT), purchasing over 5 million shares, signals a belief that in an era of AI-generated misinformation, a "trusted brand" in journalism represents a unique, durable moat [4, 8]. This move suggests that as AI commoditizes content, the "source of truth" becomes a premium asset.

In contrast, Duan Yongping interprets moats through the lens of "efficiency" and "ecosystem lock-in." His significant stake in Pinduoduo (PDD) is predicated on the company’s Consumer-to-Manufacturer (C2M) model, which uses AI-driven supply chains to eliminate intermediaries [13, 21]. Duan views this as an "efficiency moat" that is more resilient than the social or gaming moats of competitors like Tencent. For Duan, a moat is not just a barrier to entry but a mechanism for delivering superior value to the consumer at a lower cost, a principle he calls "product power" [11, 14].

### 2. Margin of Safety: Price vs. Cognition
For Buffett, the "Margin of Safety" remains largely a function of price and capital preservation. This is evidenced by his decision to build a record $381.6 billion cash pile by early 2026, divesting significant portions of Apple and Bank of America as valuations reached what he termed "casino" levels [3, 7]. Buffett’s margin of safety is found in the "defensive crouch"—waiting for a market dislocation to deploy capital into undervalued assets.

Duan Yongping, however, defines the margin of safety through "cognition." He argues that the greatest risk is "not knowing what you are doing" [11]. His margin of safety in volatile tech stocks like NVIDIA or Apple comes from a deep understanding of their product cycles and user stickiness. This cognitive depth allowed him to increase his NVIDIA stake by over 1,100% in late 2025, even as traditional value investors fled the sector due to high P/E ratios [5, 6]. To Duan, if you understand the business's "Benfen" (its core duty), the short-term price volatility is irrelevant to the long-term safety of the principal.

## II. Decision-Making Frameworks: Mental Models vs. Benfen

The intellectual frameworks used by these investors to filter information and make decisions represent two of the most influential schools of thought in modern finance.

### 1. Munger’s Latticework of Mental Models
Charlie Munger’s philosophy was rooted in "worldly wisdom"—a latticework of models from physics, biology, and psychology used to avoid "man with a hammer" syndrome. His most critical tool was "Inversion": to understand a business, one must first understand how it could fail. Before his passing, Munger applied this to AI, remaining skeptical of the hype and stating that "old-fashioned intelligence works pretty well" [16]. This skepticism remains a core part of the Berkshire culture, as seen in Buffett’s 2025 warning that AI is "part way out of the bottle" and carries the same risks as the atomic bomb [14, 15].

### 2. Duan Yongping’s 'Benfen' (Doing the Right Thing)
Duan Yongping’s framework is centered on the concept of *Benfen* (本分), which translates to "doing one's duty" or "staying focused on the right things." In an investment context, *Benfen* acts as a strategic filter that prioritizes long-term integrity over short-term gain. Duan’s application of *Benfen* involves three core pillars:
*   **Focus on the User:** Does the company provide real value? Duan’s investment in Apple is based on the belief that Tim Cook has focused on the user experience above all else [14].
*   **The 'Stop' Rule:** Duan advocates for stopping immediately when a mistake is discovered. "When you find you are wrong, you must stop immediately," he noted in a 2025 talk [14].
*   **Honesty and Transparency:** Duan avoids companies that use aggressive accounting or "casino" tactics to boost share prices, preferring those that focus on their core mission [11, 12].

## III. Portfolio Management and Risk Assessment

Both Berkshire Hathaway and Duan’s H&H International Investment practice extreme portfolio concentration, rejecting the academic standard of broad diversification.

### 1. Concentration and the 'Punch Card' Philosophy
Buffett and Duan both follow the "Punch Card" rule—acting as if they only have 20 investment slots for their entire career. As of Q4 2025, Duan’s $17.5 billion portfolio was concentrated in just 14 stocks, with the top ten holdings accounting for 99.66% of the total value [1, 3]. Apple remains the cornerstone for both, representing approximately 22.6% of Berkshire’s equity portfolio and over 50% of Duan’s [4, 5].

### 2. Risk as Permanent Loss of Capital
They define risk not as volatility (Beta), but as the permanent loss of capital. Buffett’s risk assessment in 2025-2026 has been focused on U.S. fiscal policy and the "unsustainable" budget deficit, leading him to favor cash and short-term Treasuries [8]. Duan, conversely, views the risk of "missing the future" as a form of permanent loss. This led to his tactical pivot into AI infrastructure, including new positions in CoreWeave (a GPU cloud provider) and Credo Technology, arguing that while he doesn't fully understand AI, the risk of being entirely absent from the "wheels of history" is greater than the risk of a price correction [5, 21].

## IV. The 'Duan' Adaptation: Tech and the Chinese Market

Duan Yongping’s primary contribution to the value investing lineage is his successful adaptation of Buffett’s principles to the technology sector and the Chinese regulatory environment.

### 1. Technology as a Consumer Staple
Duan was among the first to recognize that companies like Apple and Tencent are not "tech" companies in the traditional, cyclical sense, but "consumer staples" with massive switching costs. He argues that Apple’s ecosystem is the modern equivalent of Coca-Cola’s distribution network. This insight allowed him to hold Apple through multiple 30-40% drawdowns that traditional value investors could not stomach [Draft Report].

### 2. Pinduoduo and the 'Common Prosperity' Hedge
A unique aspect of Duan’s strategy is his navigation of Chinese regulatory shifts. He has characterized his investment in Pinduoduo (PDD) as a hedge aligned with China’s "Common Prosperity" goals [6, 11]. By focusing on mass-market consumption and rural digital integration, PDD serves a social function that aligns with state objectives, thereby reducing the "regulatory risk" that often plagues Chinese tech giants like Alibaba. Duan looks for companies whose business models act as a "duty-bound" service to the state's social objectives, providing a unique form of political margin of safety [11, 13].

## VI. Conclusion

The evolution of value investing from Buffett and Munger to Duan Yongping represents a transition from "Industrial Value" to "Cognitive Value." While Buffett has entered a defensive phase, retiring with a $382 billion "Cash Cathedral" to protect Berkshire’s legacy, Duan Yongping has chosen to "monetize his cognition" of the AI revolution. Duan’s adaptation proves that the core tenets of value investing—moats, margin of safety, and circle of competence—are not sector-specific but are universal principles that, when filtered through a framework like *Benfen*, can navigate even the most volatile technological and regulatory shifts of the 21st century.
